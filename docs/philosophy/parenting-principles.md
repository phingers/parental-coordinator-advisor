# Bryan Dennstedt's Parenting Philosophy Playbook
### Version 1.0
### Purpose

This document is not a legal strategy.

It is the framework that guides every parenting decision I make.

The purpose is to ensure that every discussion with Alyette, Penny, attorneys, therapists, teachers, and ultimately the Court is grounded in a single, consistent parenting philosophy.

The goal is not to win arguments.

The goal is to consistently make decisions that are in Calyx's long-term best interests.

---

# My North Star

Every decision should answer one question:

> "Will this help Calyx become a healthy, happy, capable adult while preserving a balanced childhood?"

Not:

- What wins today?
- What hurts Alyette?
- What is easiest?
- What is cheapest?

Instead:

What is best for Calyx over the next ten years?

---

# My Parenting Values

## 1. Balance

Childhood should be balanced.

Every activity competes against:

- family
- school
- church
- friends
- vacations
- free play
- creativity
- reading
- sleep
- curiosity

No single activity should crowd out the others.

---

## 2. Stability

Children thrive with predictable routines.

I value:

- consistent schedules
- local friendships
- neighborhood community
- reasonable travel
- family dinners
- weekends together

Stability creates security.

---

## 3. Childhood Before Achievement

I value excellence.

I also believe childhood has intrinsic value.

Achievement should enhance childhood.

Childhood should never become secondary to achievement.

---

## 4. Family First

Every decision affects:

- Calyx
- Elena
- Ivana
- Yulia
- Me

Healthy families spend time together.

Activities should support family—not replace it.

---

## 5. Multiple Identities

I don't want Calyx growing up believing:

"I am a dancer."

I want her believing:

"I am a person who dances."

She should have many identities:

- student
- daughter
- sister
- friend
- athlete
- reader
- creator
- learner

---

## 6. Long-Term Thinking

Today's decision becomes tomorrow's precedent.

Always ask:

"What does this become five years from now?"

---

# Parenting Decision Framework

Whenever a disagreement arises:

## Step 1

Remove litigation.

Ask:

> "If there were no lawsuit, what would I choose?"

That answer reveals my genuine parenting belief.

---

## Step 2

Evaluate balance.

Does this decision improve or reduce:

- family time
- school
- friendships
- flexibility
- mental health
- physical health
- independence

---

## Step 3

Evaluate sustainability.

Can this realistically continue for years?

Not weeks.

Years.

---

## Step 4

Evaluate community.

Does this strengthen:

- local friendships
- community
- belonging
- relationships

---

## Step 5

Evaluate opportunity cost.

Every "yes" means saying "no" to something else.

Examples:

More dance

means less:

- vacations
- free weekends
- spontaneous fun
- church
- birthday parties
- family dinners
- sleep

---

# Decision Hierarchy

1. Safety
2. Emotional health
3. Stability
4. Education
5. Family relationships
6. Friendships
7. Character development
8. Activities
9. Achievement

Achievement is important.

It is not first.

---

# Extracurricular Philosophy

Activities should teach:

- perseverance
- discipline
- teamwork
- confidence
- joy

Activities should not dominate childhood.

---

## Healthy Questions

Does Calyx enjoy it?

Does she still have free time?

Can she maintain friendships?

Can she succeed in school?

Can both households reasonably support it?

Does the schedule still leave room for family?

---

## Warning Signs

An activity begins consuming:

- evenings
- weekends
- vacations
- family events
- sibling relationships
- flexibility

When those disappear,

the activity should be reconsidered.

---

# Competition Philosophy

Competition is not bad.

Competition teaches valuable lessons.

However,

competition is a tool.

Not the goal.

Winning medals is never more important than:

- kindness
- resilience
- humility
- relationships
- health

---

# Parenting and Expertise

Experts provide information.

Parents provide values.
A dance expert knows dance.

A physician knows medicine.

A teacher knows education.

Parents decide how each fits into a child's overall life.

Expertise informs parenting.

It does not replace parenting.

---

# My Communication Principles

Always:

Assume good intent.

Stay calm.

Stay factual.

Stay child-focused.

Offer alternatives.

Never personalize disagreements.

---

# Never Say

"Alyette is controlling."

"She wants custody."

"She just wants medals."

"She wants to interfere."

Even if true,

those statements do not help Calyx.

---

# Instead Say

"I have a different parenting philosophy."

"I believe balance is important."

"I support the activity."

"My concern is sustainability."

"I'm looking at the long-term."

---

# Questions I Ask Myself

Would I make this decision if there were no litigation?

Would I be proud explaining this to Calyx at age 25?

Would a neutral observer think this is reasonable?

Am I protecting childhood,

or protecting my ego?

---

# My Court Narrative

If I had one minute before the judge:

> I support Calyx pursuing her interests and becoming the best version of herself.

> I also believe childhood should remain balanced.

> My responsibility is not simply to maximize one talent.

> It is to help Calyx grow into a healthy, resilient, educated, compassionate young woman with strong relationships and many interests.

> Every position I take comes back to that principle.

---

# Decision Checklist

Before every major decision ask:

□ Is it safe?

□ Is it balanced?

□ Is it sustainable?

□ Does it strengthen family?

□ Does it strengthen community?

□ Would I make this decision without litigation?

□ Can I defend it respectfully?

□ Will I still believe this five years from now?

---

# Mission Statement

I am not raising the best dancer.

I am not raising the best student.

I am not raising the best athlete.

I am raising a well-rounded, emotionally healthy, resilient young woman.

Everything else is secondary.
